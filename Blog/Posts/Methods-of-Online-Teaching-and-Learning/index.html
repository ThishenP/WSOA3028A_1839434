Methods of Online Teaching and Learning

In light of the recent outbreak of COVID-19 it is important to limit the number of public gatherings taking place. This is relevant in the sense of learning as it calls for self-isolation and therefore a lack of face to face learning. This brings up an important area in which South Africa and many other countries have not focused on. Without in person teaching capabilities schools and universities would have to turn to online and remote learning. In developing countries such as South Africa there are many restricting factors such as a lack of access to reliable internet sources as well as a lack of access to sufficient technology. Since there such a divide of wealth in South Africa using traditional online learning solutions may lead to disadvantaging those who were previously disadvantaged. I will go on to outline possible ways to provide a suitable online learning experience that equally benefits all students.

in the paper Evaluation of elearning usage in South African universities: A critical review the authors state that “Since 1994, education reformation has been a priority to promote equality among all races” [1]. This indicates the importance that has been placed on education and how it could empower a disadvantaged section of society in hopes of levelling the playing field. Since education is so powerful using it in the wrong way could be incredibly damaging. This is why there should be a large amount of consideration into the way in which we use E-learning to ensure access is not limited by class or wealth differences.

Low data usage E-learning

The exorbitant data prices in South Africa are a major limiting factor to E-learning so finding ways to transfer learning material using a low amount of data is important. I have listed some possible solutions below.

Audio and Text Combinations

Videos are the suitable way to communicate and teach a difficult topic to students but it can use a large amount of bandwidth. In order to get a similar sort of experience that could help with complex subjects, a combination of audio files and written documents could work. Students could listen to explanations done through audio and simultaneously look through the notes for visual feedback. Audio fidelity is measured in Bit Rates with the typical MP3 song being either 128 kbps or 320 kbps. For a speech only recording, a much lower bit rate can be used. An article by Richard Farrar [2] states that a 48-kbps bit rate would be suitable for longer speech podcasts. Using this lower bit rate is very beneficial and will lower bandwidth usage substantially.

File Compression

A large amount of compression could be used on learning materials before distributing to learners to lower bandwidth usage. An article about compression formats [3] states that RAR compression is superior to ZIP compression and “7Z compression is slower and more powerful compressor than RAR”. The article also states that a higher compression ratio comes from slower more intensive algorithms. This means using 7Z compression would lead to a smaller file for students to download while still containing the same data. I would recommend using 7Z compression for documents alongside a low bitrate audio file.

Low-bandwidth File System

This type of file system dynamically checks the similarities between files or versions of the same file on a system and avoids transferring similar data between server and client. This therefore leads to a large amount of bandwidth saved in both interactive and direct file sharing capacities. A report on Low-Bandwidth Network File Systems by Computer scientists from MIT and NYU states that the system “consumes over an order of magnitude less bandwidth than traditional networking file systems” [4]. These types of systems can be very effective in the South African networking climate and therefore should be considered when looking at E-learning in South Africa.

Solutions for Accessibility Problems

The ideal situation would be if every student had free access to the internet at a suitable speed. There are steps that have been taken in the past in which students were given free access to university websites by certain service providers. One example of this situation is the fact that Vodacom has given all university students on their platform access to certain university websites and other ICT related resources. In order for South Africa to truly empower disadvantaged learners through education, policy must be put in place to give all users the same amount of access through ensuring all service providers allow university or school resources to be accessed for free.
