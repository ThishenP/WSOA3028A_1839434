The Digital Divide in South Africa

A digital divide refers to an unequal distribution of access to ICT systems. This causes major issues in terms of the quest for equal opportunity for all. This problem is very evident in the context of South Africa and has recently been emphasised by sudden switch to online learning methods due to unforeseen circumstances. The digital divide in South Africa comes from a large wealth disparity. This disparity was formed because of apartheid and therefore disproportionately effects people of colour. The disparity alongside the lack of infrastructure provided in poorer areas is cause for such digital divide.

"It doesnâ€™t take much to see that internet access, education, skilled-employment and technological innovation are stratified along class and racial lines, thus reinforcing apartheid-era inequality."[1] This is an extract from an article titled Bridging The New Digital Divide[1]. This quote illustrates the fact that the benefits of digital access are extremely powerful and those who don't have access may be left behind which further builds up the inequalities from the apartheid era. The article also highlights the fact that digital methods are beginning to do tedious low skill jobs efficiently. This heavily affects a large group of people who have historically had much less opportunity for education due to factors outside of their control (i.e. apartheid). These groups could therefore lose the jobs that may be essential for their livelihood. The fact that the historically marginalised classes and races do not have as much access to ICT services, means that they will not reap the benefits of the new opportunities created by digital systems due to the fact that these opportunities require an understanding and familiarity with digital environments.

The capabilities of ICT systems are revolutionary and to not have access provides an extreme disadvantage. Therefore in order to grow as an equal and unified nation we have to focus on closing this gap. I feel as if in the current context of the world digital capabilities and internet access are essential in a similar way to electricity. I believe that internet should be a government provided service. This would therefore allow for a more evenly distributed spread of digital capabilities between all classes and races.
